Technology

Mining Tool

  • 50m3 Storage volume for mined materials.

- Rocket blasters
o There are eight small rocket blasters, one at each corner. These are used to match the velocity of the orbiting asteroid. They slowly bring the container down onto the asteroid and stabilise it in the zero-gravity environment. They are used again to gently propel the container off the asteroid.

o The extracted hydrogen and oxygen from the asteroid are combusted together to power the rocket blasters.

- Data Receiver
o The data receiver is a parabolic dish with an antenna that concentrates informational signals like position, conditions and materials collected back to interested parties on Earth.

- Track
o The miner has a continuous track with retractable claws that dig into the ground as the craft lands to minimise recoil in a zero-gravity environment.

- Solar panels
o Eight solar panels supply power to the batteries of the data receiver and Mobile Material Mining Unit during the time the craft is facing the sun.

- Mobile Material Mining Unit (MMMU)
o This is a compact unit that utilises laser technology to mine materials on the asteroid
We will adopt the design of JPL’S Rock climbing robot
▪ This is NASA designed technology that assists in gripping onto surfaces in zero gravity. It has many small claws that stretch, move and rotate relative to one another. It is inspired by the hooks on the legs of insects like grasshoppers.

o Laser drilling tool
▪ Lasers attached to the arms of the MMMU cut into the asteroid and scoop a 1m­­3 cylinder of material out to be analysed.

o Sensor units
▪ The craft will have external environmental sensors to map and navigate terrain and internal sensors that determine the composition of the block of mined asteroid. This data is sent back to the main container using a small dish antenna.

o Storage volume
▪ There is a 1m3 container to store the mined material until it is deposited into the container shaft.

o Water extraction unit
▪ Heaters, powered by solar panels, heat up cubes of material where ice has been detected. This melts the ice and will allow it to funnel out of the block.

o Hydrogen and oxygen (fuel) extraction unit
▪ A battery powered by solar panels is connected to the extracted water and creates an anode and a cathode. Through electrolysis, the water molecules are separated into hydrogen and oxygen gas. Although the net energy output is small, due to zero gravity, only a small amount of energy is required for takeoff and landing.

o The Panoramic camera (Pancam)
▪ Pancam is a colour imaging system designed by NASA which can rotate 360 degrees and create multispectral images

- Rocket Thrusters

o There are three rocket blasters, one underneath, and one on each opposite sides. These are used to match the velocity of the asteroid in its orbit around the sun and then slowly and effectively navigate the container down onto the asteroid to minimise bouncing due to zero gravity. They are again used to gently propel Hydrogen and oxygen will be combusted to power the blasters for both take off from base and landing on asteroid.

o Taking the extracted hydrogen and oxygen and combusting them together this powers the rocket thrusters.

- Seismic Acquisition

o An estimation of the contents of the core of the asteroid can be created by creating seismic waves which penetrate into the rock and reflect back. These waves are created with a piston which hits the ground using power from compressed air. The time it takes and angle which the waves are reflected back is picked up by sensors found on the feet of the probe and this data is gathered and model. This will provide information on densities of the rock allowing for scientists to work out what is in the core.

- Miniature Thermal Emission Spectrometer (Mini TES) – A NASA Technology Operating in the thermal infrared region of the spectrum, it identifies rock-forming minerals, and also provides some capability to see through dust coatings that could tend to obscure spectral features of rocks.

- Moessbauer Spectrometer – A NASA Technology which provides the detailed mineralogy of different kinds of iron-bearing rocks and soils. Placed against material, the spectrometer illuminates rock surfaces with gamma particles which interact with the nuclei of the atoms. characteristics of the radiation reflected back into the spectrometer are recorded and measured to determine the presence, amount and types of iron-bearing minerals.

- Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer (APXS) - A NASA technology which works by exposing materials to energetic alpha particles and X-rays and then measuring the energy spectra of backscattered alpha and X-radiation.

- The Panoramic camera (Pancam)

§ A colour imaging system designed by NASA which can rotate 360 degrees and creates multispectral images.
